About GAMABA

In today's modern world, Philippine literature and traditions help us stay connected to our roots. The GAMABA awardees, known as living treasures, keep these traditions alive through their crafts, which carry the stories, values, and identity of the Filipino people.

The Gawad sa Manlilikha ng Bayan (GAMABA) is the highest honor given to traditional folk artists in the Philippines, recognizing National Living Treasures who preserve centuries-old crafts, oral epics, and indigenous knowledge systems.

Their work shows that preserving culture is not just about objects, but also about passing down knowledge, beliefs, and customs through stories, songs, and skills. Each awardee plays an important role in keeping different Filipino traditions alive for future generations.

Featured Living Treasures

Masters of Traditional Filipino Arts

Metal Work • Pinukpuk

Eduardo Mutuc

Apalit, Pampanga

Master of pinukpuk, creating spectacular ecclesiastical metalwork with intricate baroque designs in silver and gold. His calado designs feature lace-like openwork embossed on silver-plated brass, with strikingly lifelike cherubim faces.

Spanish Colonial Baroque Religious Art Silver Plating
Mat Weaving • Sama Tradition

Haja Amina Appi

Tawi-Tawi

Revolutionary Sama mat weaver famous for her "RAINBOWS" work. She pioneered vibrant, colorful geometric patterns using wild pandan leaves, weaving from the center outward with mathematical precision and unerring color instinct.

Geometric Patterns Vibrant Colors Pandan Weaving
Gourd Hat Making • Tabungaw

Teofilo Garcia

San Quintin, Abra

Possibly the last traditional tabungaw gourd hat maker in the Philippines. His bright orange-varnished hats symbolize Ilocano resistance heritage from Diego Silang's 1762 revolt against Spanish colonial rule.

Revolutionary Symbol Natural Materials Ilocano Pride

Reflections & Literary Content

Literature as Cultural Identity

The works of Eduardo Mutuc, Haja Amina Appi, and Teofilo Garcia show how art and culture are closely connected. Their handmade works help keep Filipino traditions alive and share stories of who we are. Mutuc's metalwork mixes old Filipino methods and Spanish influences, much like the blend of cultures seen in books like Noli Mi Tangere.

Appi's woven mats use natural colors and patterns to tell stories about the Sama people's beliefs and way of life. Garcia's hats, made from gourds and rattan, show Ilocano knowledge and pride in farming. These artists do more than create crafts—they help protect our culture.

"The GAMABA award honors the Filipino traditional artists who preserve indigenous and folk artistic tradition in the Philippines. Their contributions show that literature is not limited to written texts—it also lives in songs, chants, and performances that express the identity and history of indigenous groups."

The works of the GAMABA awardees show that literature and culture are deeply connected in the Philippines. Through their crafts, these living treasures help keep alive the stories, values, and traditions of their communities. Their art proves that culture is not only preserved in books, but also in handmade creations, performances, and oral traditions passed down through generations.

Digital Netiquette

Promoting Responsible and Respectful Online Communication

Respectful Dialogue

Engage in thoughtful, respectful discussions about cultural heritage. Honor diverse perspectives and indigenous knowledge systems.

Cite Your Sources

Always credit GAMABA awardees, cultural communities, and indigenous knowledge when sharing information online.

Cultural Sensitivity

Be mindful of sacred traditions and cultural appropriation. Approach indigenous cultures with respect and humility.

No Misinformation

Share accurate information about Philippine cultural heritage. Verify facts before posting about traditional arts.
